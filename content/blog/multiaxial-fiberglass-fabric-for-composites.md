# When to Use Multiaxial Fiberglass Fabric in Composite Parts

Multiaxial fiberglass fabric is designed for composite parts that need strength in more than one direction. Instead of relying only on a traditional woven 0°/90° structure, multiaxial reinforcements can place fibers at angles such as ±45°, 0°/90°, or combined orientations to match the load path of the part.

This makes multiaxial fiberglass fabric valuable for industrial laminates, wind energy components, transportation panels, marine parts, and other composite structures.

## What Makes Multiaxial Fabric Different

A woven fabric interlaces yarns, which creates crimp. Multiaxial fabric is typically stitched, allowing fibers to remain straighter. This can improve fiber efficiency and reduce unnecessary movement during layup.

Key advantages include:

- Directional strength tailored to the part.
- Efficient laminate build-up.
- Reduced need for many separate fabric layers.
- Better control over reinforcement orientation.
- Useful handling stability from stitched construction.

## Common Fiber Orientations

Different orientations support different load cases:

| Orientation | Typical Purpose |
|---|---|
| 0° | Lengthwise tensile strength |
| 90° | Crosswise reinforcement |
| ±45° | Torsion and shear resistance |
| Multiaxial stack | Balanced structural performance |

For example, ±45° reinforcement can be useful where a part experiences twisting or shear. A 0°/90° structure is often useful for panels and beams where loads align with the part geometry.

## Where Multiaxial Fiberglass Is Used

Multiaxial fiberglass fabric may be evaluated for:

- Wind turbine blade laminates.
- Marine hulls and decks.
- Truck and rail composite panels.
- Pultrusion and infusion structures.
- Industrial covers and housings.
- Reinforced building materials.

## Selection Questions for Buyers

Before sourcing multiaxial fiberglass fabric, clarify:

1. Which load directions matter most?
2. Is the part made by hand layup, vacuum infusion, RTM, or another process?
3. What resin system is used?
4. What GSM and roll width are required?
5. Is a stitched, bonded, or combined product preferred?
6. Are surface layers or core materials part of the laminate stack?

A clear laminate target helps avoid over-specifying reinforcement and increasing cost unnecessarily.

## FAQ

### Is multiaxial fiberglass stronger than woven fiberglass?

It can be more efficient for certain load paths because fibers can be placed straighter and at selected angles. The final strength depends on orientation, GSM, resin, and laminate design.

### Is multiaxial fabric suitable for infusion?

Many multiaxial fiberglass fabrics are used in infusion processes, but wet-out and permeability should be validated with your resin and part geometry.

### Can multiaxial fiberglass reduce layup time?

Often yes. Because multiple fiber directions can be combined in one reinforcement, fewer layers may be needed compared with stacking separate fabrics.
